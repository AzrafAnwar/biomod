Introduction

Microtubules are cytoskeletal proteins used by our cells in shuttling various molecules throughout its cytoplasm. It achieves this by working in tandem with kinesin, a motor protein capable of attaching itself onto a target molecule and thereby drag it along a microtubules length. In advancing the field of nanotechnology, this could be particularly useful as microtubules can be used as tracks for carrying molecular cargo. This would require us being capable of producing microtubules long enough to serve as these tracks; the microtubules would need to be long enough to span the length of a given channel and this may stretch to the scale of several hundred of microns. In order to achieve this goal, our team spent the past summer working on developing a procedure for consistently developing long microtubules in vitro. /n To this end, we developed our procedure by basing it off of preexisting literature supporting the development of microtubules from “seeds” or tubulin that has been pre-nucleated in order to provide centers for microtubule growth. We believed that this would produce longer microtubules as the tubulin monomers present within the solution would bind on to these seeds in the absence of a nucleating agent, thereby creating a smaller number of longer microtubules.

Approach

We began developing our procedure by basing it off of pre-existing literature on microtubule seeding. In particular, following the example of Mitchison and Kirschner from their 1984 article, Dynamic Instability of Microtubule Growth, we believed that developing microtubules from pre-existing seeds would restrict sites of nucleation and growth to the seeds, thereby increasing the amount of free tubulin heterodimers binding on to the same microtubules. /n Our seeding protocol centered on the use of several different types of primary nucleating agents in growing stable seeds from which microtubules could be grown. In particular, the nucleating agents tested in the process of producing stable seeds were dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), glycerol and guanylyl-(alpha, beta)-methylene-diphosphonate (GMPCPP). In this preliminary stage of establishing a working nucleating agent for our protocol, the methods tested in developing the seeds followed the protocols provided below.
